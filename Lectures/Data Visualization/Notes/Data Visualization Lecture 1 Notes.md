##  What Is Visualization?**

> **Visualization** is the process that transforms abstract data into interactive graphical representations for the purpose of **exploration**, **confirmation**, or **presentation**.

##  Why Visualize?**

- **Communication** ‚Äì e.g., ‚ÄúWho is ahead in the election polls?‚Äù
    
- **Exploration** ‚Äì when questions are ill‚Äëdefined, such as ‚ÄúWhat is the structure of a terrorist network?‚Äù
    
- **Decision support** ‚Äì e.g., ‚ÄúWhich drug can help patient‚ÄØX?‚Äù
    

##  Purpose Spectrum**

- **Communication example** ‚Äì New York Times graphics (biased story vs. objective analysis)
    
- **Exploration example** ‚Äì Caleydo StratomeX for cancer subtype analysis
    

##  Why Graphics?**

- ‚ÄúA picture is worth a thousand words.‚Äù
    

##  When Not to Visualize (Automation)**

- **Well‚Äëdefined questions on well‚Äëdefined data** (e.g., ‚ÄúWhich gene is most frequently mutated?‚Äù)
    
- **Real‚Äëtime decisions** where human input is impractical (high‚Äëfrequency stock trading, manufacturing defect detection)

##  Why Use Computers for Visualization?**

- **Scale** ‚Äì manual drawing is infeasible for complex data (e.g., MRI scans)
    
- **Interaction** ‚Äì enables drill‚Äëdown into data
    
- **Integration** ‚Äì embeds visualizations in analysis pipelines
    
- **Efficiency** ‚Äì reusable charts across datasets
    
- **Quality** ‚Äì precise, data‚Äëdriven rendering
    
- **Storytelling** ‚Äì time‚Äëbased narratives
    

##  Why Not Rely Solely on Statistics?**

- **Anscombe‚Äôs Quartet** ‚Äì identical statistical summaries can hide vastly different patterns
    
- **Datasaurus** ‚Äì same statistics, dramatically different shapes (Matejka & Fitzmaurice, CHI¬†2017)
